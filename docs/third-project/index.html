By Adam E. Farence

Since Bitcoin’s inception in 2015, the amount of power it’s network consumes has skyrocketed from two to 124 terawatts prompting environmentalist concerns as the Bitcoin network’s power usage rivals that of Argentina, according to Cambridge University.

A terawatt contains so much power it’s only useful to describe in context of energy consumed by entire nations.

Put another way, only 31 countries out of 195 in the world that use more energy than Bitcoin. Which causes many people to wonder why so much energy is needed to power something that cannot physically be held.

The answer in part lies with the currency’s intended mission to be incredibly secure and independent of government influence. Every transaction is recorded on a ledger that everyone who is a part of the network gets a copy of. Reason being, that if someone tries to tamper with the network it becomes immediately apparent to everyone else.

Alex de Vries runs an online publication, Digiconomist, that is dedicated to airing the unintended consequences of cryptocurrency. de Vries estimates that the Bitcoin network currently uses about 77 terawatt hours a year, much lower than the 124 figure cited by the University of Cambridge. But, de Vries notes that most Bitcoin mining operations take place primarily in coal-powered regions in China.

But he also cites another concern - how much waste the Bitcoin network creates alongside its power consumption. He estimates that every year, the network generates about 10 kilo tons of waste. This waste comes from the brutal requirements Bitcoin mining imposes on machinery.
